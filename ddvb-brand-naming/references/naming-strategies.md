# Naming Strategies Catalog

When recommending naming strategies for a project, analyze the brief and competitive landscape, then recommend 2-3 strategies from this catalog. Generate seed candidates primarily in the recommended categories.

## Strategy Types

### 1. Descriptive

**What:** Name directly describes the product, service, or key attribute.
**Examples:** Salesforce, Booking.com, General Electric, PayPal, YouTube
**Russian:** СберИнвестиции, Яндекс Маркет, Почта России

**Pros:**
- Instant comprehension — no explanation needed
- Strong SEO signal
- Low marketing spend to educate

**Cons:**
- Hard to trademark (too generic)
- Limits future pivots
- Often bland, forgettable

**Best when:** Entering an established category where clarity beats creativity. B2B where decision-makers value directness. Markets where competitors use abstract names and you want to stand out with clarity.

---

### 2. Metaphorical

**What:** Name uses a metaphor, analogy, or symbolic reference to evoke a quality.
**Examples:** Amazon (vastness), Apple (simplicity), Nike (victory), Jaguar (speed/power)
**Russian:** Лавка (Яндекс — homey grocery), Точка (bank — precision)

**Pros:**
- Emotionally resonant
- Trademarkable
- Tells a brand story

**Cons:**
- Requires explanation/marketing to build association
- Risk of metaphor not landing cross-culturally
- Can feel arbitrary without supporting brand narrative

**Best when:** The brand has a strong archetype or personality. The positioning centers on an emotion or aspiration, not a product feature. Need to differentiate in a category of descriptive names.

---

### 3. Coined / Neologism

**What:** Invented word with no prior meaning. May combine morphemes from real words.
**Examples:** Spotify, Zillow, Kodak, Xerox, Skype
**Russian:** Ozon, Wildberries, АТОН

**Pros:**
- Universally trademarkable
- Domain availability (if truly novel)
- Blank canvas for brand meaning
- Works across languages

**Cons:**
- Zero inherent meaning — requires massive marketing investment
- Risk of sounding meaningless or tech-bro
- Hard to spell/remember if poorly constructed

**Best when:** Global ambitions (name must work in 3+ languages). Well-funded brand launch. Category where all obvious names are taken. Tech/innovation positioning.

**Construction techniques:**
- Morpheme combination: Insta + gram, Micro + soft
- Sound symbolism: hard consonants (K, T) = strength; soft (L, M) = warmth
- Truncation: Cisco (San Francisco), Fedex (Federal Express)
- Latin/Greek roots: Audi (hear), Volvo (I roll)

---

### 4. Acronym / Initialism

**What:** Letters from a longer name, now used standalone.
**Examples:** IBM, BMW, H&M, KFC, DHL
**Russian:** БКС, ВТБ, МТС

**Pros:**
- Short, compact
- Professional/institutional feel
- Works when the full name is unwieldy

**Cons:**
- No inherent meaning
- Hundreds of existing 3-letter acronyms
- Forgettable unless backed by massive awareness
- Hard to trademark (many conflicts)

**Best when:** Company has an established long name and wants to modernize. B2B/institutional context where brevity signals professionalism. The acronym happens to spell something meaningful (ASUS, NASA).

**Warning:** Do NOT recommend as primary strategy for new brands. Acronyms work for established names transitioning, not for blank-slate naming.

---

### 5. Portmanteau / Blend

**What:** Fuses two words into one, retaining parts of each.
**Examples:** Pinterest (pin + interest), Instagram (instant + telegram), Microsoft (microcomputer + software)
**Russian:** Яндекс (yet another indexer, loosely)

**Pros:**
- Combines two meaningful concepts
- Often unique enough to trademark
- Can be catchy and memorable

**Cons:**
- Can sound forced if poorly blended
- May be confusing if component words aren't obvious
- Limited to concepts that blend phonetically

**Best when:** Two strong brand concepts need to merge (speed + reliability, local + global). Need a coined-feeling name with more inherent meaning than pure neologism.

---

### 6. Founder / Patronymic

**What:** Named after a person — founder, inventor, or symbolic figure.
**Examples:** Tesla, Disney, Ford, Siemens, Bosch
**Russian:** Касперский, Тинькофф (now T-Bank)

**Pros:**
- Human, personal, builds trust
- Usually unique and trademarkable
- Heritage/legacy signal

**Cons:**
- Tied to one person's reputation (risk)
- Can feel dated or small-scale
- Doesn't scale to multi-product empires easily

**Best when:** The founder IS the brand story. Consulting, luxury, craft businesses. Heritage and trust are key purchase factors.

---

### 7. Geographic / Place-Based

**What:** Named after a location — real or aspirational.
**Examples:** Amazon, Patagonia, Adobe (Adobe Creek), Cisco (San Francisco)
**Russian:** Байкал (water), Камчатка (adventure)

**Pros:**
- Evokes place associations (quality, origin, character)
- Often unique enough to trademark
- Can signal local pride or global aspiration

**Cons:**
- May limit geographic expansion
- Associations may not translate across cultures
- Can feel arbitrary if the place connection isn't clear

**Best when:** Origin story matters (local production, regional expertise). The place evokes the right associations. Export markets value the geographic signal.

---

## Recommending Strategies

After analyzing the brief and competitive landscape, recommend 2-3 strategies. Present as:

```
Based on the brief and competitive analysis:

**Primary recommendation: Metaphorical**
Why: [1-2 sentences linking to positioning, archetype, competitive landscape]

**Secondary: Coined/Neologism**
Why: [1-2 sentences]

**Avoid: Descriptive, Acronym**
Why: [1-2 sentences explaining why these don't fit]
```

## Seed Generation Guidelines

When generating seed candidates:

1. **Generate 30-50 total** across recommended strategies
2. **Label each candidate** with its strategy type
3. **Provide etymology** for each (what roots, what the name evokes)
4. **Note cross-language behavior** for each (how it sounds in target languages)
5. **Flag phonetic risks** (negative associations, hard to pronounce, confusable)
6. **Group by strategy** for easy comparison

Format:
```
## Metaphorical (15 candidates)

1. **[Name]** — [etymology/meaning]. [Cross-language note]. [Phonetic note].
2. **[Name]** — ...

## Coined (10 candidates)

1. **[Name]** — [construction: root1 + root2]. [Cross-language note].
```
